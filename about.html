Introduction

I am an independent researcher working on collapse in recursive digital systems — formal claim boundaries and falsifiable benchmarks. My current work centers on Loopzero, the framework I built: a claim boundary specified in Lean 4, and an empirical bridge evaluated on two frozen public benchmarks under a matched false-positive contract.

The work is deliberately narrow: characterizing the conditions under which recursive systems enter a no-progress regime — where local updates remain coherent but meaningful forward improvement becomes inaccessible.

Research orientation

After Meta Reality Labs, I spent an extended period reading widely on the attention economy — its history, and the feedback architectures that define contemporary digital platforms. What kept drawing my attention was that the failure modes recurring in some of these systems were described only informally, even where they appeared to share a structure. The question that pulled me in was a narrow one: could one such failure mode be specified precisely enough to be tested and contested, rather than only described?

My background in art history and cultural theory gave me one way to see the problem; working at scale inside recommendation and discovery architectures gave me another. Loopzero is my attempt at that specification — a precise claim boundary in Lean 4 and an empirical bridge tested under a defined protocol. So far it establishes a falsifiable framework and directional evidence, not a finished detector; that boundary is the point, and I keep it explicit.
