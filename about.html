Introduction

I am an independent researcher working on collapse in recursive digital systems — formal claim boundaries and falsifiable benchmarks. My current work centers on Loopzero, the framework I built: a claim boundary specified in Lean 4, and an empirical bridge evaluated on two frozen public benchmarks under a matched false-positive contract.

The research is focused on a specific and bounded problem: characterizing the conditions under which recursive systems enter a no-progress regime where local updates remain coherent but meaningful forward improvement becomes inaccessible.

Research orientation

After Meta Reality Labs, I spent an extended period studying the history and structure of the attention economy — from early mass-market print media through behaviorism, early AI, and the feedback architectures that define contemporary digital platforms. The hypothesis that drove the work was that the collapse patterns recurring across these systems have a formally describable structure — one regularly encountered in practice but otherwise described only informally. My background in art history and cultural theory gave me one set of tools; working at scale inside recommendation and discovery architectures gave me another. Loopzero is my attempt to provide that specification: a precise claim boundary in Lean 4 and an empirical bridge that can be tested under a defined protocol.
